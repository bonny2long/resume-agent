# Why I Switched to Software Engineering

## The Realization

After 6+ years as an Electrical Technician and Heat & Frost Insulator, I hit a ceiling. I was good at my job - I could diagnose complex electrical systems, manage multi-million dollar insulation projects, and lead teams effectively. But I kept thinking: "What if I could solve problems for thousands of people at once instead of one building at a time?"

## The Turning Point

At Alexander Dennis Inc., I was working on diagnostic software for electrical systems. I realized the software was more powerful than my hands - it could diagnose issues across entire fleets, not just one vehicle. That's when it clicked: software was the ultimate troubleshooting tool.

## The Decision Process

1. **Skills Inventory**: I listed my transferable skills
   - Systems analysis (electrical → software systems)
   - Project management (construction → software projects)
   - Technical documentation (schematics → code documentation)
   - Team leadership (field crews → dev teams)

2. **Research**: I investigated i.c Stars vs other options
   - i.c Stars offered business + leadership + tech
   - Focused on practical, project-based learning
   - Strong network and career placement

3. **Risk Assessment**: 
   - Financial: Could I afford the transition?
   - Time: How long would it take?
   - Success Rate: Was I technical enough to succeed?

## The Leap

I quit a stable, well-paying union job with benefits to study full-time at i.c Stars. It was terrifying - I had a family depending on me. But I believed the long-term payoff was worth it.

## Results So Far

**Self-Taught Phase (Sep 2024 - Aug 2025)**: Built first full-stack apps, learned React/Node.js, created SyncUp and Chef BonBon projects
**i.c. Stars Phase (Aug 2025 - Dec 2025)**: Completed intensive 4-month program, led United Airlines AI platform project
**Current goal**: Become the kind of developer who bridges physical and digital worlds

## Advice to Others in Similar Situation

1. **Inventory Your Skills First**: You probably have more transferable skills than you realize
2. **Start Learning While Working**: Build projects in evenings/weekends to test your interest
3. **Network Early**: Talk to people who made similar transitions
4. **Pick the Right Program**: Look for practical, project-based learning, not just theory
5. **Embrace Your Background**: Your previous experience makes you unique, not less qualified

## Why It Was Worth It

- **Impact**: My code now helps thousands of users instead of one building
- **Growth**: Learning is constant and exciting
- **Compensation**: Higher ceiling with remote work flexibility
- **Satisfaction**: Building things that didn't exist before

But the best part? I still get to be a troubleshooter - just with code instead of tools.
